On Damnatio Memoriae
Damnatio memoriae was a Roman legal instrument — the formal condemnation of a person's memory. Names were chiselled from monuments. References were erased from official records. Historians were instructed to omit. Constantine I applied it to his own son Crispus and his own wife Fausta in 326 CE — within his own lifetime, in the same year as the executions. The man whose symbol became the banner of Western civilization, the curriculum of Western education, and the moral authorization for the Doctrine of Discovery used the erasure tool on his own family before using it on the historical record. The title of this document uses Constantine's own instrument.
The Primary Source Record — Constantine I · What the Documents Say
Context
Constantine I (272–337 CE) is celebrated as the first Christian Roman Emperor, the man who issued the Edict of Milan (313 CE) establishing religious tolerance, convened the First Council of Nicaea (325 CE), and put the Chi-Rho on the battle banner at the Milvian Bridge (312 CE). He is venerated as a saint in the Eastern Orthodox Church. The following is what the primary source record — not the official biographies, which omit it — documents about the man whose symbol became the authorization for conquest.
Primary Source 1 — Execution of Crispus · 326 CE
"In 326 AD, Constantine ordered his firstborn son Crispus to be put to death. Crispus was executed with 'cold poison.' The emperor ordered the names of the two executed to be removed from all records, not to be mentioned by historians, and their memory erased forever."
Source: Greek Reporter, citing Ammianus Marcellinus, Sidonius Apollinaris, Zosimus · December 9, 2025 · greekreporter.com
Primary Source 2 — Execution of Fausta · 326 CE
"A few months later, Fausta was also executed. They locked her in a bath and gradually raised the temperature until she died of suffocation."
Source: Greek Reporter, citing Zosimus, Epitome de Caesaribus · December 9, 2025 · greekreporter.com
Primary Source 3 — The Erasure · Eusebius
"Their names were wiped from the face of many inscriptions, references to their lives were eradicated from the literary record, and their memory was condemned. Eusebius edited out any praise of Crispus from later copies of Historia Ecclesiastica, and his Vita Constantini contains no mention of Fausta or Crispus."
Source: Wikipedia — Constantine the Great, citing Eusebius of Caesarea · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great
Primary Source 4 — The Official Silence
"It is noteworthy that most of Constantine's contemporary Christian biographers exclude this incident completely when recounting his life."
Source: Gloria Romanorum — Constantine's Execution of Crispus and Fausta · gloriaromanorum.blogspot.com · citing Philostorgius, Ecclesiastical History
Primary Source 5 — The Battle of Milvian Bridge · Head on a Spike
"At the Battle of the Milvian Bridge, Constantine faced and defeated his wife's brother Maxentius. Maxentius in his attempt to escape drowned in the river Tiber and by order of Constantine his head was cut off, nailed to a tree and the soldiers paraded it through the streets of Rome."
Source: Greek Reporter · December 9, 2025 · greekreporter.com
